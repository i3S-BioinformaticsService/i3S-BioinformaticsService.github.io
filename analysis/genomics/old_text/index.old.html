Background

1. Alignment strategy

1.1 De Novo Sequencing

The goal of de novo genome assembly is to determine the sequence of a genome using only randomly sampled sequence fragments. This technique is usually employed on novel genomes when a reference or template sequence is not available. In de novo assembly, random fragments from a genome are sequenced and computationally stitched together to generate sequence contigs and scaffolds. The key aspects for achieving high quality de novo assemblies, particularly on highly repetitive genomes, are read length, overlap mapping quality and assembly algorithm. High quality genome assemblies usually employ both short-read and long-read genome sequencing stratagies.

1.2 Resequencing

Resequencing is typically performed when a reference genome sequence is available. Sequencing reads are aligned back to the reference to determine the location in the genome the specific read best matches. Resequencing is often applied to variant detection (single nucleotide polymorphisms, small insertions/deletions, structural variants, copy number variation) and derivatives such as tumor vs normal comparison, population genetic analysis, Mendelian disease analysis, and trio sequencing.

Q2. How much coverage do I need?

The answer is highly dependent on many factors, including the goals of your experiment, the organism of interest, and the amount of material available. Below are some generalized recommendations:

Short-read genome sequencing
  • Germline/frequent variant analysis: 20-50x
  • Somatic/rare variants: 100-1000x
  • Tumor vs Normal: ≥100x tumor, ≥30x normal
  • De novo assembly: 100-1000x
Long-read genome sequencing:
  • Gap filling and scaffolding: 10x
  • Large structural variant detection: 10x
  • Germline/frequent variant analysis: 20-50x
  • De novo assembly: 50-100x

Q3. How do I calculate coverage?

Coverage is a multiplier based on the total size of the genome (see below). For humans, 30x coverage can be achieved with 600 million reads of 150 bp (or 300M paired-end reads). Please note that nowadays companies advertise coverage as the number of gigabases given, meaning that 600M reads of 150bp corresponde to 90Gbp (600 x 106 reads x 150 bp/read / 109). By estimating based on gigabases instead of coverage the company is protecting themselves against the fact that not all reads sequenced will align against the target genome.

  • Coverage = (read length) x (total number of reads) / (genome size)
  • Example: 30x = (150 bp/read) x (600 x 106 reads) / (3 x 109 bp)

1.3 Genotyping by microarray

Microarrays genotyping is a common and cost-efficient strategy to detect single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) or larger structural changes of known markers in a species genome. These microarrays can containg million of markers and allow for customization of the array content and are commonly used for high-throughput, multiplex processing for large-scale population studies, clinical research. Although there are microarray solutions for non-human genomes, human microarrays are the most predominant.
